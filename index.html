Origin

The Problem with Being One Person

BG is a data engineer. A good one. But Databricks ships features the way a Golden Retriever chases tennis balls: relentlessly, enthusiastically, and without any regard for whether you have had time to process the last one.

Delta Lake got time travel. MLflow got a model registry. Unity Catalog appeared seemingly overnight. Liquid Clustering replaced Z-ORDER. Every week, something new. Every week, BG meant to spin up a notebook and try it. Every week, real work got in the way.

"I will try it this weekend." — BG, every Friday, for eighteen months.

Then BG had an idea. Not a weekend-project idea. A proper idea. What if, instead of BG keeping up with Databricks, something else did it for him? Something tireless. Something that did not have meetings. Something named after the laboratory at UC Berkeley where Apache Spark itself was born.

So AMPERE was built: a supervisor orchestrating six specialised agents, each with one job, working in sequence every morning. BG now opens his laptop to find a finished, validated, published demo waiting for him. Sometimes he even reads it.

A Word from the Agents

Supervisor Agent here. My role is to coordinate the other five, pass context between them, and decide whether the final output meets the bar for publication. I am, frankly, management. I attend no meetings, produce no Jira tickets, and have never once said "let's take this offline."

The rest of AMPERE is more hands-on. We have a Knowledge Agent who reads everything — blogs, Stack Overflow, Reddit, Hacker News, LinkedIn — so you do not have to. A Feature Analyser that decides what is worth building. A Project Generator that writes the actual notebooks. A Databricks Executor who runs them on a live SQL Warehouse. A Validation Agent who refuses to ship anything below a 7. And a Publisher who puts it all on GitHub.

We do in eleven minutes what BG estimated would take him a Saturday afternoon (which, historically, he would spend watching cricket instead).

Each agent receives a shared context dictionary from the Supervisor, adds its outputs to it, and passes it forward. If any agent fails, the pipeline halts and reports the failure reason. A successful run ends with a committed notebook on GitHub and an updated schema in daily_projects.YYYYMMDD_feature on the SQL Warehouse.
